What Is a Stock? (In Simple Terms)

A stock is partial ownership in a company.

When you buy a stock, you are buying a small piece of a real business. That ownership gives you a proportional claim on the company's assets and future profits.

If you plan to invest, you should understand exactly what you own.

No opinions. No hidden assumptions. Just arithmetic.

What Does Owning a Stock Mean?

Owning a stock means you own a fraction of a company.

If a company has 1,000,000 shares outstanding and you own 1,000 shares:

1,000 / 1,000,000 = 0.1%

You own 0.1% of the business.

  • Company assets
  • Company earnings
  • Future growth

Ownership is mathematical.

What Is a Share of Stock?

A share is a unit of ownership in a company.

If total company value is: $100,000,000
And there are: 1,000,000 shares

$100,000,000 / 1,000,000 = $100 per share

Stock price reflects total company value divided by shares outstanding.

How Do Stocks Make Money?

Stocks generate returns in two ways:

  1. Price appreciation
  2. Dividends

1. Price Appreciation

If you buy at $50 and sell at $70:

$70 - $50 = $20

This is a capital gain.

2. Dividends

Some companies distribute a portion of their profits directly to shareholders called dividends.

If a company pays $2 per share annually and you own 100 shares:

100 × 2 = $200

You receive $200 in cash and still own the shares.

How Dividends Affect Stock Price

Dividends redistribute existing value.

If a $100,000,000 company pays $2 per share across 1,000,000 shares:

2 × 1,000,000 = $2,000,000

New company value becomes $98,000,000.

$98,000,000 / 1,000,000 = $98 per share

Total economic value remains unchanged.

Why Do Stock Prices Go Up and Down?

  • Earnings reports
  • Interest rate changes
  • Economic conditions
  • Investor expectations

Stock prices reflect the present value of expected future cash flows.

Are Stocks Risky?

Stocks fluctuate. Short-term volatility can be significant.

Long-term returns depend on growth, reinvestment, and time.

Summary

A stock is partial ownership in a company.

  • Claim on assets
  • Claim on profits
  • Exposure to future growth

Stocks generate returns through price appreciation and dividends.

The arithmetic is simple. The discipline is not.
